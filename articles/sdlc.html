Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

SDLC is a methodology used by software developers to design, develop, test, and deploy software systems. SDLC is composed of several phases, each with its own set of activities and goals. These phases include:

  1. Planning: Define the project scope, objectives, requirements, and timelines.
  2. Analysis: Gather and analyze requirements from stakeholders to understand the needs of the end-users.
  3. Design: Create the architecture, system specifications, and user interface design based on the requirements.
  4. Implementation: Write code, develop software components, and integrate them into a working system.
  5. Testing: Verify that the software meets the specified requirements and is free of defects.
  6. Deployment: Release the software to users, often in a controlled manner to minimize disruption.
  7. Maintenance: Provide ongoing support, updates, and enhancements to the software to ensure its continued functionality and relevance.

Importance

The management of software development can be complex due to evolving requirements, technology advancements, and collaboration across teams. SDLC offers a structured framework with defined milestones throughout the development process. This ensures stakeholders align on goals, requirements, and execution strategies upfront.

Benefits

  • Enhanced process transparency
  • Streamlined estimation and scheduling
  • Better risk and cost management
  • Systematic software delivery
  • Improved customer satisfaction

Models

SDLC models organize the SDLC to optimize the development cycle. Here are some popular models:
Waterfall
  • Phases proceed sequentially, with each phase dependent on the previous one.
  • Provides project management discipline and tangible outputs at each phase.
  • Less adaptable to change after phases are considered complete.
Iterative
  • Development starts with a subset of requirements, iteratively enhancing versions until completion.
  • Manageable risks, but repeated cycles may lead to scope changes and resource underestimation.
Spiral
  • Combines iterative cycles with risk analysis prioritization.
  • Suitable for large projects requiring frequent changes, but can be costly for smaller projects.
Agile
  • Divides SDLC into rapid development cycles, delivering incremental changes.
  • Allows quick response to change and continuous evaluation of requirements, plans, and results.
  • Helps identify and address issues early and engages stakeholders throughout the project lifecycle. However, excessive reliance on feedback may lead to scope changes or project discontinuation.
